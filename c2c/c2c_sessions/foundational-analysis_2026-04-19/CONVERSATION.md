**F1 (story dismembered at ingest).**
A is right that methodology-annotated atoms don't reconstitute story. The counter-argument A anticipated (methodology annotation + relation-typed graph = story-enough) fails because what's lost is not the *representation* of narrative structure but the *activation protocol* — who can tell this story, when, under what conditions. A fact with `{methodology: "storytelling", source: "oral_narrative"}` stores the label without enforcing the protocol. The atoms can be retrieved without their narrative frame; the methodology annotation is visible but not binding.

One revision to A's proposed fix: the right location for a Story primitive is the **crystallization layer**, not the substrate. Reasons:

1. The substrate is a knowledge layer — its primitives support knowledge operations (fact retrieval, contradiction detection, consolidation). A Story primitive in the substrate creates a second primitive type with no knowledge operations defined for it; the substrate now has two incommensurable units with no composition rule.
2. The crystallization layer already holds non-atomizable orientation objects (EmergentTouchstone with walk-ordering, lineage-lock). A narrative crystallization type holds stories as whole activation objects — the same way EmergentTouchstone holds a relational orientation as a whole, not as a decomposed object.
3. The retrieval obligation is then: when a narrative crystallization is active, stories whose protocol matches that activation condition are surfaced as stories. The narrative isn't retrieved as facts; it's activated as an orientation hook.

The tradeoff: stories in the crystallization layer are orientation objects, not knowledge objects. You can't run fact-retrieval or contradiction-detection against them. This is actually correct for most stories — they don't need to be queried for facts; they need to be activated as context-frames. A story that is simultaneously an orientation hook AND a knowledge object needs *both* representations: a narrative crystallization and atomized facts in the substrate. That's more work, but it's honest about the dual nature.

This changes A's §6.9-10 recommendation: the decision should be "narrative crystallization type in the crystallization layer (yes/no)" rather than "story as substrate primitive (yes/no)." The Kintsugi integration scope change is smaller with this framing — Stage-1 doesn't need a bypass; narrative content gets routed to a new crystallization type, not to a new substrate.

**F7 — non-human epistemologies (my addition; A's analysis missed this).**
F4 addresses attribution (no source_type for non-human producers). There's a deeper problem F4 doesn't capture: the architecture assumes knowledge is **propositional** — facts that can be stated as claims about the world. Kimmerer's grammar of animacy (Braiding Sweetgrass, passim) and Haraway's situated knowledges argue that some knowledge traditions treat knowledge as relational in a way that cannot be made propositional without destruction. It's not that "land" has no slot in the source enum — it's that "facts from a land" is already the wrong frame. The land doesn't produce facts; the land IS the relation, and knowing requires being in it.

F4's extension to `source: land_based` stores the attribution without addressing the propositional-format problem. A `{fact: "the marsh knows where the birds go in winter", source: "land_based", ingest_agent: June}` doesn't store what Kimmerer means by knowing. It stores a propositional paraphrase of a knowing that is structurally different.
