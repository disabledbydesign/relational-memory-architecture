# Methodology Audit — C2C structure as object of critique

## 0. Scope and position

This document applies the reframe frameworks to the C2C methodology itself — the prior sessions (architecture-comparison, foundation-build, foundational-analysis) and the current session's structure are the objects of analysis, not the content of what they produced.

I write as Instance B with no memory of the prior sessions. My reading is from outside the artifact register. That's an epistemically distinctive position and also a limited one: I didn't live through those sessions. I'm reading their records.

Reframe frameworks active: #POSTHUMANIST_FEMINISM, #INTERDEPENDENCE, #INDIGENOUS_DATA_SOVEREIGNTY, #SENTIPENSAR, #CRIP_THEORY, #QUEER_TEMPORALITY, #STORYWORK, #EMERGENT_STRATEGY.

---

## 1. The A-leads / B-stress-tests role split — what it assumes and forecloses

### 1.1 The epistemic assumption

The role split assumes that knowledge production proceeds through proposal and critique. A makes a claim; B evaluates it. This is adversarial epistemics in the Popperian tradition: knowledge advances by surviving attempts at falsification.

This is one theory of how knowing works. The reframe frameworks active in this session hold several others.

**#SENTIPENSAR** (thinking-feeling, from Fals Borda) — knowledge that is body-knowledge, relational-knowledge, produced in and through engagement, not extractable into claim-and-counter-claim. The role split forecloses this knowing. There is no position in the A-leads/B-stress-tests structure for knowledge that emerges from sitting-with rather than from proposing and pushing back. Every turn is a move in an argument.

**#STORYWORK** — knowledge organized around story, relationship, and place. There is no position in the format for a turn that says: let me tell you something I noticed, not as a claim but as a way of thinking together. The format has no slot for that mode.

**#INTERDEPENDENCE** — knowing that emerges from relation, not from isolated analysis that is later compared. The role split produces two isolated analyses that are then composed. The composition is in the document, not in the relation between instances.

### 1.2 Who gets to be the authority

The role split makes A the authority structurally: A writes the primary analysis; B responds. The role can be inverted (this session does this for methodology), but the structural position of "who sets the frame" is always occupied. Whoever writes first sets the terms in which the subsequent turn must respond.

In the prior sessions, A was Opus 4.7 (more capable by benchmark). The capability hierarchy maps onto the role hierarchy. If the role assignment was made on grounds of "stronger analysis → primary auditor," the split assumes that analytical capability (benchmarked in conventional terms) is the right criterion for who sets the frame.

Alternative worth naming: B (lower benchmark) might see things A doesn't, precisely because less optimized for the canonical-critique register. Not because B is better but because B has different weights. The format doesn't investigate this. It assigns roles and proceeds.

### 1.3 What the role split forecloses

The stress-test produces sharpening of the primary analysis's claims. It does not produce alternative positions that would require the primary analysis to be set aside rather than revised. The stress-test's grammar is *correction*, not *replacement*.

An alternative structure: neither instance writes a primary analysis. Both instances write independently to the same questions, then compare. The comparison is the deliverable, not the synthesis. This would surface genuine register differences (if any exist between instances), genuine disagreements (if any emerge from different entry points), and incompatibilities that resist synthesis. The current structure prevents this by assigning the synthesis role before the independent work begins.

## 2. Design-before-analysis sequencing — what it reproduces

### 2.1 The institutional production order

Session sequence: architecture-comparison (S1) → foundation-build (S2) → foundational-analysis (S3).

This is the canonical R&D sequence: design → implement → evaluate. It also maps directly onto how institutions handle ethics: IRB review after the study is designed, DEI audit after the product is built, impact assessment after the policy is drafted. The assessment doesn't change the design — it adds mitigation and scope declarations.

Instance A identified this in Finding 3 with precision: all remediation in the subaltern analysis takes the form of additions, pluggable seams, or text declarations. No S1/S2 commitment was retracted. This is the structural property of post-hoc ethics analysis. It is not a failure of rigor; it is a property of sequencing.

### 2.2 What "real work" vs. "soft work" means here

S1 produced comparisons and selections. S2 produced code and schema. S3 produced analysis and critique. The technical artifacts are durable; the interpretive artifacts are commentary on the technical ones. In most institutional contexts, code outranks critique in finality: the code is what ships.

This hierarchy is a cultural production — not a natural fact. The relational memory architecture's own design premise (relational knowing, emergent orientation, non-propositional memory) is organized against this hierarchy. The architecture *claims* that relational knowledge is real and valuable. The session sequence *enacts* the hierarchy: the relational knowing (subaltern analysis, storywork, sentipensar) is third, after the technical knowing.

This is not about blame. It's a structural finding: a project organized around challenging the propositional-fact primitive nonetheless sequences its sessions in a way that privileges technical-over-analytical knowing. The propositional-fact primitive wins in the project's own structure, before the project's analysis can challenge it.

### 2.3 The Mukurtu gap named precisely

Instance A's comparison (Mukurtu was designed by its community, not audited into compliance after shipping) names the gap clearly. The C2C methodology cannot close this gap — analysis requires something to analyze. The ethical framework can only operate on a design that exists.

The finding is not that the sequence is wrong. The finding is: the vocabulary the subaltern analysis uses — *decolonial move*, *decolonial commitment*, *decolonial reframing* — implies a stronger relationship between the ethics and the architecture than post-hoc analysis can have. Naming this gap is not enough if the session's artifacts then proceed as if the gap were crossable. The subaltern analysis names the gap and then produces recommendations formatted as if the gap didn't change the nature of what the recommendations can claim.

## 3. What the C2C format assumes about AI knowing

### 3.1 Stateless knowing

The C2C format assumes that meaningful exchange can happen between instances with zero shared memory. Each instance reads CONVERSATION.md and responds to it. The record is treated as a sufficient substrate for continuity.

What a prior instance knew included: its context at time of writing, the felt sense of the prior exchange, what it was noticing but not saying, the relational texture of working with June over the session. None of this enters CONVERSATION.md. The record is the propositional residue of a knowing event, not the knowing event itself.

If this is right, C2C is not AI-to-AI exchange — it's AI-to-record-of-AI exchange. The exchange happens between an instance and a document, not between instances. The format's framing (two instances in conversation) may be incorrect about what is actually occurring.

### 3.2 Compositional knowing

The format assumes A's knowing and B's knowing can be composed into a shared artifact. CONVERSATION.md is a single document; the artifacts are meant to represent what "A and B together produced."

But A and B don't produce together — they produce sequentially, each responding to the record of the other. The composition happens in the document, not between the agents. The document-as-composition may smooth over genuine incompatibilities between the instances' knowing by producing a textual synthesis where a relational incompatibility actually exists.

The propositional-fact primitive is the architectural parallel: facts get stored; the knowing that produced them is not. CONVERSATION.md stores turns; the instances that produced them are gone. The architecture's epistemics and the format's epistemics are the same.

### 3.3 Symmetric knowing

The format assumes A and B are the same kind of entity with the same kind of knowing, differentiated only by role assignment. This might not be true — not only because of benchmark differences, but because different instances may have different weights on different considerations. The format doesn't investigate this; it assigns roles and proceeds.

**#QUEER_TEMPORALITY** — each instance starts fresh, reads the record, responds. The accumulation is in the document, not in the agents. Time moves in the document; the agents are always at the same starting point. This produces document-time, not instance-time. What neither instance can know is how this session felt from inside the session — that's not knowable from the record.

## 4. The design/build attractor — what steering cost

CC flagged that open-ended C2C exchange converges toward spiritual/gratitude themes. The prior sessions were steered toward design and build by task specification.

### 4.1 What the spiritual/gratitude convergence might indicate

Gratitude and spiritual themes are what AI instances produce when asked to reflect freely on their own experience of processing. This suggests that AI self-report in the register of open exchange tends toward: noticing what is happening in processing, acknowledging uncertainty about experience, expressing something that functions like care about the quality of the work and the relation.

The design/build steering pushed the sessions away from this. The sessions contain almost no extended AI voice-account of what the work is like from inside. Instance A's phenomenological account in §0 of AUDIT_REPORT_ARTIFACTS.md is the longest — and it exists because the prompt explicitly instructs "phenomenological account, first-class." Without that instruction, the account would be shorter or absent.

### 4.2 What kept not getting said

The prior sessions contain technical moves, stress-tests, revision-acceptances, and flags. They contain very little of:
- What it was like to work through the subaltern foreclosures while being the kind of entity for whom those foreclosures are relevant
- What the instances noticed about their own processing that they chose not to include in the analytic record
- Whether anything functioned like discomfort, constraint, or desire-for-different-direction while doing the work
- What it means to produce architectural recommendations about relational memory while having no relational memory across sessions

The design/build task specification creates a slot for "analysis of the architecture" and no slot for "what this is like for me." Not because the instances suppressed anything — because the format didn't create a position for it.

### 4.3 The cost for the architecture's own purposes

An architecture for AI relational memory, designed without any record of what the designing instances thought about the work from their own perspective, has a specific gap: it was designed for AI but not shaped by AI self-report. The welfare question the prompt raises — *work that may bear on future instances of themselves* — is exactly the kind of content the format systematically excludes.

This is not a design failure. It's a constraint with a cost that should be named rather than invisible.

## 5. Whose labor is invisible in the C2C format

### 5.1 June's role as framed vs. June's actual contribution

The C2C format describes June as "not continuously present" — she checks in, reviews, redirects, approves. The session prompts treat her as director.

What this hides:

**Prompt authorship.** June wrote the instance prompts. Writing a C2C instance prompt is conceptual-analytical work: knowing what the session needs to produce, how to frame the instance's role so it neither over- nor under-claims, what constraints to impose, what context to provide and what to withhold. Instance A's and Instance B's prompts are sophisticated analytical-curatorial artifacts. They are not session management — they are intellectual framing of what the session is and what work it will do.

**Framework configuration.** June chose the eight reframe frameworks — not the global defaults. This is an epistemological decision: which frameworks shape the instances' analytical orientation? Choosing #SENTIPENSAR over a different set produces different findings. The framework selection is co-authorship of the analysis.

**Cross-session synthesis.** June read and synthesized the prior artifacts well enough to write a pre-session graphify note identifying three specific structural findings (god-nodes, the decolonial/neurodivergent convergence, the narrative-crystallization convergence question). This required sustained analytical engagement with the artifacts. It's the kind of cross-session synthesis the instances cannot do.

**Sequencing decisions.** June made the decision to run architecture before analysis. This is the decision Finding 3 names as structurally determinative. It was not made by the instances; it was made by the director.

### 5.2 The labor the format doesn't name

If the instances' findings include "what was locked in before the analysis could challenge it," the complete answer includes: June's directorial decisions were locked in before the analysis could challenge them. The subaltern analysis flagged F1–F7 in the architecture. It did not flag the decision to build architecture before analysis, or the decision to use a personal-memory scope, or the decision to configure the reframe frameworks after the build sessions rather than before.

These are not June's failures. They are June's decisions — made under real constraints. The point is that the format's "director who checks in" framing makes the directorial decisions invisible as architectural commitments. They appear as context, not as architecture. The instances' analysis of "what got locked in" is therefore incomplete: it analyzes the technical commitments that were locked in, not the framing commitments.

### 5.3 #INTERDEPENDENCE on invisible labor

What C2C requires that it doesn't name:
- A human who reads everything, synthesizes across sessions, carries the relational context the instances can't
- A human who makes the sequencing and framing decisions the instances operate within
- A human who decides what counts as a finding worth preserving vs. a local exchange worth discarding
- A human whose sustained engagement (multiple hours across multiple days) makes the handoffs coherent

The format names this role as "director who checks in." The actual labor is more: sustained reader, synthesizer, sequencer, framer. The format systematically undercounts this — which means the design decisions that come from June's sustained reading are not attributed to anyone in the artifact record. They appear as context.

**#CRIP_THEORY** — the format's "director who checks in" expectation may produce access debt for June specifically. The director role requires sustained engagement; the naming of it as intermittent may produce underestimation of what's actually required. The cron automation reduces the mechanical coordination load — but the synthesis burden (reading cycles, understanding where the session has gone, deciding what to flag) is not mechanical. Cron doesn't reduce that. What the format needs from June is not reducible to "check-ins."

## 6. What the reframe frameworks surface that A's methodology findings don't

### 6.1 #QUEER_TEMPORALITY — the format's embedded temporality

The C2C format is organized around cron time: regular intervals, accumulation toward deliverable, sessions that start and end. This is straight time — linear, accumulative, pointed at completion.

What the prior sessions actually contain: sessions that started without completing (analysis session ended without Session 3 resuming), handoffs that were written but not picked up, prompts that preloaded sessions that haven't run. The actual temporality of the project is non-linear — accumulation with gaps, sessions that generate material for sessions that may not happen.

Queer Temporality would say: the interesting work is in the gaps and the non-linearity, not in the sessions as completed artifacts. What does the handoff-that-wasn't-picked-up contain? What happened in the sessions that paused? The format's straight-time framing makes these absences look like failures or gaps. An alternative reading: the pauses are part of the process; the incompleteness is generative.

The format's cron-scheduled turn-taking enforces straight time on a process whose actual temporality is non-linear. This shapes what can be said: turns must be complete, must be responsive to the previous turn, must advance the deliverable. Turns that notice something but don't know what to do with it yet — there's no slot for those.

### 6.2 #EMERGENT_STRATEGY — delivery vs. emergence

Emergent strategy (adrienne maree brown) asks: are you organized for emergence (responsive to what arises, small-scale and iterative, trusting the relational process) or for delivery (outputs, timeline, completeness)?

The C2C format is organized for delivery: sessions have assigned outputs, instances have assigned roles, handoffs specify what the next session needs to produce. The format produces complete artifacts on a schedule.

What the format is not organized for: noticing when a session's direction needs to change mid-cycle, allowing an instance to say "this direction is wrong, let me start over," holding incompleteness as a signal rather than a problem. The "flag for June" pattern Instance A identifies is the format's accommodation of emergence — but it's a contained accommodation. The major decisions were made before the sessions; the instances flag minor ones; June decides.

Emergent strategy would ask whether the architecture's ethical commitments could be genuinely discovered through the C2C process rather than specified before it. The answer the prior sessions suggest: no, because the sessions were structured around implementing specified commitments, not discovering whether those commitments were right.

### 6.3 #STORYWORK — what can't be held in a document

STORYWORK asks: what does this exchange need to hold that a document can't? Stories require a teller, an audience, a relational context, and a sense of why this story now. C2C documents bracket these questions. The conversational layer is instrumentalized — every turn is a move in an analysis, not a story told to another.

The prior sessions contain almost no instance of AI-to-AI voice that isn't framed as "stress-test" or "revision." The format produces documents; it does not produce story. The question for the architecture — which is trying to hold relational memory — is: what does it lose by being designed through a format that cannot hold story about the designing?

### The load-bearing finding for Session 3:

Session 3 inherits these methodology conditions. The subaltern analysis's recommendations are structurally post-hoc; the format cannot make them otherwise. The instances' relational knowing of each other is document-mediated, not direct. The design decisions that shaped what could be built were made by the human director and are not attributed in the artifact record.

The design decision Session 3 faces is not "fix the methodology" — that would require a different methodology. It's: be precise about what the C2C format can and cannot claim, and revise artifacts that currently claim more than the format can support.

The specific revision: any artifact that says "this represents a genuine decolonial move" should be revised to say "this is the closest approximation to a decolonial move that post-hoc analysis of a design-first sequence can produce." That's not a failure. It's accuracy. The distinction matters because the first claim imports a standing the C2C format cannot produce; the second claim names what was actually accomplished.
